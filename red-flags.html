Common Red Flags

Learn to spot the warning signs of a phishing attack.

Urgent Language

Creates a sense of panic to make you act quickly.

Generic Greetings

Uses "Dear Customer" instead of your actual name.

Suspicious Sender

The email address looks wrong or has misspellings.

Mismatched Links

The link URL doesn't match the link's text.

Spelling/Grammar Errors

Professional companies usually don't have bad grammar.

Unexpected Attachments

Never open attachments you weren't expecting.

1. Check the Sender and Address

  • Suspicious Sender Address: The email address might look almost right but have subtle misspellings, extra numbers, or an unusual domain name (e.g., amazon@service-update.net instead of service@amazon.com).
  • Generic Greeting: The message uses a generic greeting like "Dear Customer," "Sir/Madam," or "Valued User," even if it claims to be from an organization that knows your name.
  • Unexpected Request: You receive an unexpected email, especially if it asks to "verify" your account. Legitimate companies rarely ask you to do this via an unsolicited email link.

2. Analyze the Content

  • Urgency and Threats: The message uses dramatic language to pressure you, such as "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours" or "Legal action will be taken." Scammers want to override your critical thinking.
  • Unusual Grammar or Spelling: While AI is making this less common, persistent spelling errors, poor grammar, or awkward phrasing can be a major red flag.
  • Requests for Sensitive Information: Any email that directly asks you to reply with your password, PIN, or full credit card number is a scam.

3. Scrutinize Links and Attachments

  • Hover to Discover: On a desktop, hover your mouse over any link *without clicking*. The actual destination URL will appear in the corner of your browser window. If the link URL doesn't match the sender's website, do not click it.
  • Mismatched URLs: The link text might say "Click here" but the URL is completely different or contains a misspelling (e.g., micros0ft.com instead of microsoft.com).
  • Unexpected Attachments: Never open an email attachment from an unknown sender, especially if it has a suspicious file extension like .exe, .zip, or is a document asking you to "Enable Macros."
