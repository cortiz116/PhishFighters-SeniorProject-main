Defense & Recovery

Implement best practices and learn what to do immediately if you've been phished.

Best Practices to Avoid Phishing

By implementing these best practices, you can significantly reduce your risk of falling victim to a phishing attack.

Protect Your Accounts and Devices

  • Use Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Enable MFA on all your sensitive accounts such as email, banking, social media. This requires a second form of verification like a code sent to your phone, making it extremely difficult for a thief to log in, even if they steal your password.
  • Use Strong, Unique Passwords: Create long, complex passwords that combine letters, numbers, and symbols. Never reuse the same password across multiple accounts. A password manager can help you manage unique passwords securely.
  • Keep Software Updated: Enable automatic updates for your operating system, web browser, and security software. Updates often include critical security patches that protect against new threats.
  • Install Antivirus/Anti-Malware Software: Use reputable security software and keep its virus definitions up-to-date to scan for and remove malicious files.

Safe Online Behavior

  • Never Click the Link: If a message is suspicious, do not click the link or open the attachment. Instead, open a new browser window and type the company's official website address directly into the address bar.
  • Verify Independently: If you receive a warning about an account problem (like a bank, credit card, utility bill, etc.), contact the company using a known, trusted method like the phone number on the back of your card or their official website. **Do not** use the contact information provided in the suspicious message.
  • Check for HTTPS: Before you enter any sensitive information on a website, ensure the URL starts with https:// and a padlock icon is present in the address bar. This indicates the connection is encrypted and more likely to be secure.
  • Be Careful with Public Wi-Fi: Avoid conducting sensitive activities like online banking or shopping while connected to unsecured public Wi-Fi networks. Consider using a VPN for extra protection.

What to Do If You've Been Phished

If you realize you have clicked a suspicious link or entered credentials on a fake site, act immediately:

  • Change Passwords: Immediately change the passwords for the compromised account and any other account that uses the same password.
  • Monitor Accounts: Review your bank and credit card statements for any unauthorized transactions. Contact your financial institutions immediately if you see anything suspicious.
  • Scan Your Device: Run a full scan using your anti-malware software to check for and remove any malware that may have been downloaded.

Report the Phish

  • Forward phishing emails to the Anti-Phishing Working Group at reportphishing@apwg.org.
  • Report the incident to your organization's IT or security team if it was a work-related account.
