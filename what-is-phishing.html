What is Phishing?

Learn how attackers use deception to steal your sensitive information.

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of social engineering attack where a criminal, masquerading as a trusted entity like a bank, co-worker, or government agency, attempts to trick you into revealing sensitive information. This information can include usernames, passwords, credit card numbers, or other financial data.

The term "phishing" comes from the idea that attackers use an enticing "lure" to "fish" for a victim's personal data.

How Phishing Works

The attack usually follows a few simple steps:

  • The Lure: The attacker sends a fraudulent message—often an email, text, or call—that appears legitimate and creates a sense of urgency, fear, or excitement.
  • The Hook: The message asks you to take an action, such as clicking a link, opening an attachment, or replying with personal information.
  • The Catch: If you click the link, you're often taken to a fake website that looks identical to a real one. Any information you enter on this fake site is stolen by the attacker. If you open an attachment, it may install malware on your device.

Common Types of Phishing

Email Phishing

The most common form, using fake emails to lure victims.

+

More Details:This is the classic "bulk" phishing attack, sent to millions of people. It often impersonates large, well-known companies like Microsoft, Google, or your bank, using generic greetings.

Smishing

Phishing attacks sent via SMS text messages.

+

More Details: Smishing uses text messages to create urgency. You might get a text about a "package delivery issue" or a "suspicious login" with a link. These are especially dangerous because people tend to trust texts more than emails.

Vishing

Voice phishing done over the phone with urgent requests.

+

More Details: Vishing (Voice Phishing) involves a phone call. The attacker might be a live person or an automated "robocall" claiming to be from the IRS, Amazon, or tech support, trying to scare you into giving up information.

Spear Phishing

A highly targeted attack aimed at a specific individual or organization.

+

More Details: Unlike bulk phishing, a spear phishing email is customized. The attacker will research you online (e.g., from LinkedIn) and use your name, job title, or a colleague's name to build trust and make the email seem highly credible.

Whaling

Spear phishing that specifically targets high-profile executives (C-suite).

+

More Details: Whaling is spear phishing for "big fish." An attacker might impersonate the CEO and email the CFO with an "urgent, secret" request to wire money to a new vendor (which is actually the attacker's account).

Angler Phishing

Phishing that uses fake social media accounts to impersonate a company.

+

More Details: Attackers create fake social media accounts (e.g., a fake support page for a brand) and "angle" for customers who post public complaints, offering to "help" them via a link to a fake support site.

Clone Phishing

An attack that copies (clones) a legitimate, previously-sent email.

+

More Details: The attacker copies a real email (like a newsletter or update) and sends an "updated" version. They'll claim the original had a "broken link" and provide a new, malicious link to click.
